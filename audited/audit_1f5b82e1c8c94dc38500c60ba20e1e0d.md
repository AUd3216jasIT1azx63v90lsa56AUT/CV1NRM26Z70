### Title
Reentrancy via `repay`/`liquidate` Callback Allows Theft of Other Lenders' Withdrawable Tokens — (File: src/Midnight.sol)

---

### Summary

Both `repay` and `liquidate` in `Midnight.sol` increase `marketState[id].withdrawable` before pulling loan tokens from the caller, and invoke an attacker-controlled external callback in between. Because `withdraw` is callable during that window and the contract has no reentrancy guard, an attacker who controls the callback and holds credit in the market can re-enter `withdraw`, drain tokens from the protocol's existing balance (belonging to other lenders), and then satisfy the post-callback `safeTransferFrom` with a smaller amount — netting a profit at other lenders' expense.

---

### Finding Description

**Root cause — `repay` (lines 508–520):** [1](#0-0) 

The sequence is:
1. **Line 509** — `withdrawable += units` (state written, tokens not yet received)
2. **Lines 514–519** — `IRepayCallback(callback).onRepay(...)` — external call to attacker-controlled contract
3. **Line 520** — `safeTransferFrom(loanToken, payer, address(this), units)` — tokens finally pulled

During step 2, `withdrawable` is inflated by `units` relative to the protocol's actual token balance. The `withdraw` function is fully callable during this window: [2](#0-1) 

`withdraw` decrements `withdrawable` and immediately transfers tokens from the protocol's balance. Because `withdrawable > balance` during the callback, the re-entrant `withdraw` draws from tokens that belong to other lenders, not from the tokens that will arrive in step 3.

**Same pattern in `liquidate` (lines 675–717):** [3](#0-2) 

`withdrawable += repaidUnits` at line 675, collateral is sent to receiver at line 696, then `ILiquidateCallback(callback).onLiquidate(...)` is called at lines 698–714, and loan tokens are only pulled at line 717.

**No reentrancy guard exists.** The only transient-storage protection is `liquidationLocked`, which exclusively prevents liquidation of the seller during `take` callbacks: [4](#0-3) 

It does not protect `repay` or `liquidate` callbacks from re-entering `withdraw` or any other state-mutating function.

---

### Impact Explanation

**Theft of other lenders' tokens.**

Invariant broken: `protocol_balance(loanToken) >= withdrawable`. During the callback window this invariant is violated by exactly `repaidUnits`. A re-entrant `withdraw` exploits this gap to transfer tokens that belong to other lenders out of the protocol. After the callback completes and the payer satisfies `safeTransferFrom`, the invariant is numerically restored, but the stolen tokens are gone and the remaining `withdrawable` pool is permanently short — other lenders can no longer withdraw their full entitlement.

Maximum theft per attack: `min(attacker_credit, existing_withdrawable) − repaidUnits`.

Severity: **High** — direct, irreversible theft of user funds with no privileged access required.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

**Realistic and low-barrier.**

Attacker preconditions (all achievable by any unprivileged user):
1. Deploy a contract that implements `IRepayCallback` and `IWithdrawCallback` logic.
2. Have that contract accumulate credit in the target market (by taking a buy offer — a normal protocol flow).
3. Wait for other users to repay debt, building up `withdrawable` in the protocol (or seed it themselves with a separate account).
4. Call `repay` with the malicious callback.

No admin keys, no oracle manipulation, no flash-loan dependency required. Any market with active lenders and repayments is a valid target.

---

### Recommendation

Apply the **checks-effects-interactions** pattern strictly: move `safeTransferFrom` (the token pull) to **before** the external callback in both `repay` and `liquidate`. Alternatively, add a transient-storage reentrancy lock (analogous to `liquidationLocked`) that guards all state-mutating entry points during any active callback execution.

For `repay`, the corrected order should be:
1. Update state (`debt -= units`, `withdrawable += units`)
2. Pull tokens from payer (`safeTransferFrom`)
3. Invoke callback (now the invariant is already restored)

For `liquidate`, the corrected order should be:
1. Update all state
2. Pull loan tokens from payer (`safeTransferFrom`)
3. Transfer collateral to receiver
4. Invoke callback

---

### Proof of Concept

**Setup:**
- Market M with loan token T.
- Lender A has previously repaid debt; protocol holds **1 000 T** with `withdrawable = 1 000`.
- Attacker deploys `AttackerContract` which implements `IRepayCallback`.
- `AttackerContract` has previously lent tokens and holds **1 500 credit** in market M.
- A borrower `B` has **500 debt** in market M (controlled or uncontrolled by attacker).

**Attack call:**
```
Midnight.repay(M, 500, B, address(AttackerContract), "")
```

**Execution trace:**

| Step | Action | `withdrawable` | Protocol Balance T |
|------|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 | `position[B].debt -= 500` | 1 000 | 1 000 |
| 2 | `withdrawable += 500` | **1 500** | 1 000 ← gap created |
| 3 | `AttackerContract.onRepay(...)` called | — | — |
| 4 | ↳ re-enters `withdraw(M, 1000, AttackerContract, attacker)` | 500 | 0 (1 000 T sent to attacker) |
| 5 | callback returns `CALLBACK_SUCCESS` | 500 | 0 |
| 6 | `safeTransferFrom(T, AttackerContract, Midnight, 500)` | 500 | 500 |

**Result:**
- Attacker received **1 000 T**, paid **500 T** → **net profit: 500 T**
- Lender A's withdrawable entitlement reduced from 1 000 to 500 → **500 T stolen from Lender A**
- Protocol balance (500) = withdrawable (500) — invariant appears restored, theft is concealed in accounting
