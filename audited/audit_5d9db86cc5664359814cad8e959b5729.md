### Title
Missing `rcfThreshold > 0` validation allows markets where dust positions are permanently un-liquidatable in normal mode — (File: src/Midnight.sol)

---

### Summary

`touchMarket()` validates LLTV, `maxLif`, collateral ordering, and maturity upper-bound, but performs **no validation on `market.rcfThreshold`**. Any permissionless market creator can set `rcfThreshold = 0`. When this happens, the Recovery Close Factor (RCF) dust-deactivation branch inside `liquidate()` becomes permanently unreachable, trapping slightly-unhealthy dust positions in a state where no liquidator can profitably close them before maturity. The resulting bad debt is socialized across all lenders in that market.

---

### Finding Description

**Root cause — no lower-bound check in `touchMarket()`**

`touchMarket()` enforces several invariants on market parameters: [1](#0-0) 

`lltv` is checked against a whitelist of nine non-zero values; `maxLif` is checked against two computed formulas; maturity has an upper bound. `market.rcfThreshold` receives **no check at all** — it can be `0`.

**How `rcfThreshold` is consumed in `liquidate()`**

In normal (pre-maturity) mode the RCF enforcement block is: [2](#0-1) 

The `require` passes if **either**:
1. `repaidUnits <= maxRepaid` — the liquidator stays within the RCF cap, **or**
2. `collateral_value/LIF − maxRepaid < rcfThreshold` — the remaining collateral is dust, so the RCF is deactivated and a full liquidation is allowed.

When `rcfThreshold = 0`, condition 2 becomes:

```
uint256_value < 0   →   always false  (uint256 arithmetic)
```

Only condition 1 can ever be true. The RCF is therefore **permanently active** — no full liquidation is possible in normal mode regardless of how small the remaining collateral is.

**Why this creates bad debt**

`maxRepaid` is computed as:

```solidity
uint256 maxRepaid = lltv < WAD
    ? (_position.debt - maxDebt).mulDivUp(WAD * WAD, WAD * WAD - lif * lltv)
    : type(uint256).max;
```

For a position that is only slightly unhealthy (`debt − maxDebt` is tiny), `maxRepaid` is tiny. If the gas cost of the liquidation transaction exceeds the incentive earned on `maxRepaid` units, no rational liquidator will act. The position stays unhealthy, the collateral price can continue to fall, and by the time post-maturity mode becomes available the shortfall may be large — all of which is socialized among lenders via `lossFactor`. [3](#0-2) 

---

### Impact Explanation

- Dust positions in a `rcfThreshold = 0` market cannot be profitably liquidated before maturity.
- Unhealthy positions accumulate; collateral price can deteriorate further during the remaining TTM.
- At or after maturity, post-maturity mode (which skips the RCF check entirely) becomes available, but by then the bad debt may be substantial.
- Bad debt is socialized among all lenders in the market via `lossFactor`, causing direct, measurable loss of lender credit. [4](#0-3) 

---

### Likelihood Explanation

Market creation is **fully permissionless** — `touchMarket()` is a public function callable by any address. A malicious market creator can deliberately set `rcfThreshold = 0` to create a market where dust positions are immune to pre-maturity liquidation, then attract lenders. Alternatively, an honest creator who does not understand the parameter's role may set it to `0` by omission (zero is the Solidity default for unset `uint256` fields). Neither scenario requires any privileged key. [5](#0-4) 

---

### Recommendation

Add a non-zero check for `rcfThreshold` inside the market-creation branch of `touchMarket()`:

```solidity
require(market.rcfThreshold > 0, InvalidRcfThreshold());
```

This mirrors the existing pattern used for other safety-critical parameters and ensures the dust-deactivation path in `liquidate()` is always reachable.

---

### Proof of Concept

**Setup**
1. Attacker calls `touchMarket()` with a valid `Market` struct where `rcfThreshold = 0` and all other parameters are legitimate (valid LLTV, valid `maxLif`, future maturity).
2. Lenders supply credit by taking buy offers in the new market.
3. Borrower (attacker or third party) supplies collateral and takes sell offers to borrow.

**Trigger**
4. Oracle price drops slightly — borrower's position becomes unhealthy by a small margin (e.g., `debt − maxDebt = 1 wei`).
5. `maxRepaid` is computed as `1.mulDivUp(WAD*WAD, WAD*WAD - lif*lltv)` — a dust amount.
6. Liquidator calls `liquidate()` with `repaidUnits > maxRepaid` (attempting a full close).
7. The RCF deactivation condition evaluates `something < 0` → `false`.
8. `require(repaidUnits <= maxRepaid || false)` → **reverts** with `RecoveryCloseFactorConditionsViolated`.
9. Liquidator tries `repaidUnits = maxRepaid` (dust). Gas cost > incentive earned → economically irrational; no liquidator acts.

**Outcome**
10. Position remains unhealthy for the entire pre-maturity window.
11. Collateral price continues to fall; bad debt grows.
12. After maturity, post-maturity mode allows full liquidation, but the bad debt is now large and is socialized among lenders via `lossFactor`, causing direct loss of lender credit. [6](#0-5) [7](#0-6)
