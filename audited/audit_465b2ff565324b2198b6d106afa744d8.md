### Summary
When a position carries bad debt and a liquidator calls `liquidate` in `postMaturityMode` with `seizedAssets > 0` before `TIME_TO_MAX_LIF` has elapsed, the bad debt reduction (line 628) uses `maxLif` to shrink `_position.debt`, while `repaidUnits` (line 650) is computed with the smaller `lif`. Because `lif < maxLif`, `repaidUnits` per unit of collateral exceeds the remaining debt ceiling, and the checked subtraction at line 676 reverts with an arithmetic underflow. No guard exists in the `postMaturityMode` branch to cap `repaidUnits` at `_position.debt`.

### Impact Explanation
Any liquidator calling `liquidate(seizedAssets=S > 0, postMaturityMode=true)` on a position that has bad debt and where `block.timestamp < market.maturity + TIME_TO_MAX_LIF` receives an unconditional revert. The `seizedAssets`-input liquidation path is completely broken for this combination of conditions. The position can still be liquidated via the `repaidUnits`-input path or by first realising bad debt with a `(0,0)` call, but the primary collateral-seizure entry point silently fails, delaying or preventing timely liquidation. ### Likelihood Explanation
**Preconditions:**
1. A position has bad debt (price dropped below `debt / (collateral * maxLif / ORACLE_PRICE_SCALE)`).
2. The market has passed maturity but fewer than 15 minutes have elapsed (`lif < maxLif`).
3. A liquidator calls with `seizedAssets > 0`.

All three conditions are reachable without any privileged action. Bad debt is a normal protocol outcome; the 15-minute ramp window is a fixed constant. Any liquidator who uses the collateral-input form of the call (the natural choice when targeting a specific collateral amount) will hit this. The window is short (15 min) but the revert is deterministic within it. [6](#0-5)
