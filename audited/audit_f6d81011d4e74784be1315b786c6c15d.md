### Title
Lenders Can Frontrun Pending Bad-Debt Liquidations to Escape Loss Socialization — (File: src/Midnight.sol)

---

### Summary

`withdraw()` applies the current `lossFactor` at call time via a lazy `_updatePosition()`. Because `lossFactor` is only updated inside `liquidate()` when the liquidation transaction executes, any lender who sees a bad-debt liquidation in the mempool can frontrun it with a `withdraw()` call, exit at the pre-slash rate, and shift their entire share of the bad-debt loss onto the remaining lenders.

---

### Finding Description

**Root cause — lazy, snapshot-based slashing**

Bad debt is socialized by updating `lossFactor` atomically inside `liquidate()`: [1](#0-0) 

Individual lender positions are **not** slashed at that moment. The slash is applied lazily the next time `_updatePosition()` is called for that lender, which reads the current `lossFactor` from storage: [2](#0-1) 

`withdraw()` calls `_updatePosition()` as its very first action: [3](#0-2) 

If the lender's `withdraw()` lands **before** the liquidation, `_updatePosition()` sees the old (pre-slash) `lossFactor`, applies zero slash, and the lender exits at full credit value. After the liquidation executes, `lossFactor` rises based on the now-smaller `totalUnits`, amplifying the slash on every remaining lender.

**End-to-end exploit path**

1. Market state: `totalUnits = 1 000`, `withdrawable = 500`, `lossFactor = 0`.
2. Lender A holds `credit = 100`; Lender B holds `credit = 900`.
3. A liquidation with `badDebt = 100` appears in the mempool.
4. Lender A submits `withdraw(units = 100)` with higher gas.
   - `_updatePosition` reads `lossFactor = 0` → no slash.
   - `totalUnits` drops to `900`; `withdrawable` drops to `400`.
5. Liquidation executes:
   - New `lossFactor` is computed over `totalUnits = 900`, `badDebt = 100`.
   - Lender B's effective credit is slashed by `100 / 900 ≈ 11.1 %`.
6. **Without** the frontrun both lenders would each bear `10 %`; with it Lender A bears `0 %` and Lender B bears `≈ 11.1 %`.

No privileged access is required. The only preconditions are that the attacker already holds credit in the market and that `withdrawable ≥ units` (i.e., some prior repayment has occurred).

---

### Impact Explanation

A lender who monitors the mempool can **systematically avoid every bad-debt loss** in any market they participate in, transferring their share of each loss entirely to passive lenders. Because the attacker's early withdrawal also reduces `totalUnits` before the liquidation, the `lossFactor` increment is larger than it would otherwise be, compounding the harm to remaining lenders. The attacker's gain equals their proportional share of the bad debt that they escaped.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

MEV infrastructure (flashbots, private mempools, searcher bots) routinely monitors for profitable frontrun opportunities. A pending liquidation with bad debt is a clear, on-chain-visible trigger. The preconditions — holding credit and having some `withdrawable` in the market — are normal operating conditions for any active lending market. The attack is repeatable across every bad-debt event in every market.

---

### Recommendation

Apply the slash eagerly at the time of `liquidate()` rather than lazily at the next `_updatePosition()` call, or record a "pending slash checkpoint" that is enforced before any `withdraw()` can proceed. Concretely, one approach is to call `_updatePosition` for all affected lenders inside `liquidate()` (expensive but precise), or to store the `lossFactor` at which each lender last interacted and revert `withdraw()` if the market's `lossFactor` has changed since the last block (a simpler circuit-breaker). At minimum, the protocol should document this as a known risk so integrators can build protective wrappers.

---

### Proof of Concept

```
State before attack:
  totalUnits   = 1000
  withdrawable = 500
  lossFactor   = 0
  Lender A: credit = 100, lastLossFactor = 0
  Lender B: credit = 900, lastLossFactor = 0

Pending mempool tx: liquidate(..., badDebt = 100)

Step 1 — Lender A frontruns with withdraw(units = 100):
  _updatePosition reads lossFactor = 0  →  no slash
  credit[A]    = 0
  totalUnits   = 900
  withdrawable = 400
  Lender A receives 100 loan tokens at full face value.

Step 2 — Liquidation executes:
  _totalUnits = 900, badDebt = 100
  new lossFactor = MAX - (MAX - 0) * (900 - 100) / 900
                 = MAX - MAX * 800/900
                 ≈ MAX * (1/9)   ← ~11.1% slash

Step 3 — Lender B's next interaction calls _updatePosition:
  postSlashCredit = 900 * (MAX - MAX/9) / MAX = 900 * 8/9 = 800
  Lender B loses 100 units (≈ 11.1%) instead of the fair 90 units (10%).

Lender A's avoided loss: 10 units (their fair share).
Extra loss imposed on Lender B: 10 units.
