### Title
Blacklisted staker's principal permanently locked with no recovery path — (`src/ConfidencePool.sol`)

### Summary
If a staker's address is blacklisted by the stake token (e.g., USDC) after depositing but before claiming under a SURVIVED or EXPIRED resolution, their principal is irrecoverably locked in the pool. No sweep or admin path can recover it.

### Finding Description
`claimSurvived()` and `claimExpired()` both transfer tokens unconditionally to `msg.sender` via `stakeToken.safeTransfer(msg.sender, payout)`. [1](#0-0) [2](#0-1) 

If the stake token implements an address blacklist (USDC, USDT, etc.) and the staker's address is added to that blacklist after staking but before claiming, every call to `claimSurvived()` or `claimExpired()` by that staker will revert at the `safeTransfer` line. Because the claim is `msg.sender`-gated, no third party can claim on their behalf.

The only potential escape valve — `sweepUnclaimedBonus()` — explicitly reserves `totalEligibleStake` (which still includes the blacklisted staker's amount, since `totalEligibleStake` is only decremented inside the claim functions themselves) and sweeps only the excess above that reserve: [3](#0-2) 

Because `totalEligibleStake` is never decremented for the blacklisted staker, `reserved` always covers their principal, `amount` is always zero for that portion, and `sweepUnclaimedBonus()` can never touch it. The funds are permanently stranded.

### Impact Explanation
A staker who is blacklisted by the stake token after depositing loses their entire principal with no on-chain recovery path. The pool's accounting permanently reserves those tokens (`totalEligibleStake` never reaches zero for that user), so neither the staker, the sponsor, nor any permissionless caller can retrieve the locked amount. The loss is bounded to the blacklisted staker's own deposit, but it is total and irreversible.

### Likelihood Explanation
Requires two sequential events: (1) the factory owner allowlists a token with a blacklist capability (USDC is the canonical example and a realistic choice for a staking pool), and (2) the staker's address is added to that blacklist between their `stake()` call and their post-resolution claim. Both events are individually plausible; their conjunction is uncommon but not negligible, especially given that whitehat attackers (who may also be stakers) are exactly the class of addresses that token issuers sometimes blacklist following a high-profile exploit.

### Recommendation
Add a pull-over-push fallback: if `safeTransfer` to the claimant fails, record the owed amount in a `pendingClaims[address]` mapping and allow the sponsor or a permissionless keeper to sweep unclaimed principal to `recoveryAddress` after a timeout (analogous to the existing `sweepUnclaimedBonus` pattern). Alternatively, wrap the outbound transfer in a try/catch and credit the amount to `recoveryAddress` immediately on failure, mirroring the approach suggested in the referenced report.

### Proof of Concept
1. Factory owner allowlists USDC as a stake token.
2. Alice calls `stake(1_000e6)` — USDC is transferred in; `eligibleStake[Alice] = 1_000e6`, `totalEligibleStake += 1_000e6`.
3. The pool resolves SURVIVED; `snapshotTotalStaked` is captured.
4. USDC issuer blacklists Alice's address (e.g., following a regulatory action).
5. Alice calls `claimSurvived()`. Execution reaches `stakeToken.safeTransfer(msg.sender, payout)` and reverts — USDC rejects the transfer to a blacklisted recipient.
6. `hasClaimed[Alice]` remains `false`; `eligibleStake[Alice]` and `totalEligibleStake` are unchanged.
7. Any caller invokes `sweepUnclaimedBonus()`. `reserved = totalEligibleStake` still includes Alice's 1 000 USDC; `freeBalance - reserved ≤ 0` for Alice's share; the sweep cannot touch her principal.
8. Alice's 1 000 USDC is permanently locked in the contract with no on-chain recovery path.
