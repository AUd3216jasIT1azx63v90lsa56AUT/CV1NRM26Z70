Audit Report

## Title
Cross-chain signature replay via identical EIP-712 domain separator on chains sharing the same `chainid` - (`File: src/ratifiers/EcrecoverRatifier.sol`)

## Summary
`EcrecoverRatifier.isRatified` constructs its EIP-712 domain separator exclusively from `block.chainid` and `address(this)`. When two chains share the same `chainid` and the contracts are deployed at the same addresses (standard with deterministic CREATE2 deployments), the domain separator is byte-for-byte identical on both chains. A maker's signature produced on chain A is therefore cryptographically valid on chain B, enabling an attacker to replay it and force the maker into a loan position they never authorized.

## Finding Description

**Root cause — `src/ratifiers/EcrecoverRatifier.sol` line 40:**
```solidity
bytes32 domainSeparator = keccak256(abi.encode(EIP712_DOMAIN_TYPEHASH, block.chainid, address(this)));
```
The domain separator has exactly two inputs: `block.chainid` and `address(this)`. If both are identical on two chains, the separator is identical and any signature valid on one chain is valid on the other.

**Why existing guards do not stop the replay:**

- `isRootCanceled[maker][root]` (`EcrecoverRatifier.sol` line 21) is per-chain storage. A root canceled on chain A has no effect on chain B.
- `consumed[offer.maker][offer.group]` (`Midnight.sol` line 191) is per-chain storage. Consumption on chain A does not affect chain B.
- The `Offer` struct contains no chain-specific nonce or chainid field; `hashOffer` encodes only market/trade parameters.
- `Midnight` captures `INITIAL_CHAIN_ID = block.chainid` at construction (`Midnight.sol` line 205). If both chains share the same `chainid`, `INITIAL_CHAIN_ID` is identical on both, so `IdLib.toId` (`IdLib.sol` line 25-31) produces the same market IDs when `Midnight` is deployed at the same address, making the replayed `Offer.market` reference valid on chain B.

**Exploit flow:**
1. Chain A and chain B share `chainid = X`. `EcrecoverRatifier` and `Midnight` are deployed at the same addresses on both chains via deterministic deployment.
2. Maker signs an offer tree root on chain A. Digest: `keccak256("\x19\x01" || keccak256(EIP712_DOMAIN_TYPEHASH, X, ratifier_addr) || structHash)`.
3. Attacker extracts `(sig, root, leafIndex, proof)` from chain A calldata or mempool.
4. Attacker calls `Midnight.take` on chain B with the maker's offer and the extracted `ratifierData`.
5. `EcrecoverRatifier.isRatified` computes the domain separator on chain B: `keccak256(EIP712_DOMAIN_TYPEHASH, X, ratifier_addr)` — identical to chain A.
6. `ecrecover` recovers the maker's address; `_signer == offer.maker` passes.
7. The take executes: the maker's credit is consumed on chain B, the taker receives loan tokens, and a debt position is opened against the maker.
8. If the borrower's collateral depreciates below LLTV, the position becomes undercollateralized and bad debt accrues against the maker's credit on chain B.

## Impact Explanation
An unprivileged attacker can force a lender's offer to be filled on a chain the lender never signed for. The lender's funds on chain B are locked into a borrow position they did not authorize. If the borrower's collateral depreciates, the lender suffers direct, unrecoverable bad debt — a concrete violation of the protocol invariant that signatures bind the right user/market/action/chain. This constitutes unauthorized movement of assets and potential permanent loss of lender funds.

## Likelihood Explanation
**Required preconditions:**
- Two chains sharing the same `chainid`: a documented real-world occurrence (multiple L2 testnets have historically shared chainids with other networks; EIP-155 non-compliance on some chains).
- Deterministic deployment at the same address: standard practice for DeFi protocols using CREATE2 with a fixed salt and deployer.
- Maker having a funded position on chain B: required for bad debt to materialize; plausible if the protocol is live on both chains simultaneously.

The attack is fully repeatable for every offer root the maker has signed, until the maker explicitly cancels the root on chain B — which they may never do if unaware of the chain B deployment. No privileged access is required; any external caller can submit the replayed `take`.

## Recommendation
Cache the domain separator at construction time using a chain-specific value that cannot collide across deployments, or include an explicit chain identifier beyond `block.chainid`. The most robust fix is to follow the standard EIP-712 pattern and include the contract's `name`, `version`, and `verifyingContract` fields in the domain separator, and additionally bind the domain separator to a deployment-specific salt or the `Midnight` contract address (which already encodes `INITIAL_CHAIN_ID` in its market IDs). Concretely:

```solidity
// In constructor, cache:
DOMAIN_SEPARATOR = keccak256(abi.encode(
    EIP712_DOMAIN_TYPEHASH,
    keccak256("EcrecoverRatifier"),
    keccak256("1"),
    block.chainid,
    address(this)
));
```

And if cross-chain deployment is expected, add a deployment-unique salt (e.g., the `Midnight` address, which already encodes `INITIAL_CHAIN_ID`) to further differentiate deployments on chains that share a `chainid`.

## Proof of Concept
**Minimal fork test plan:**
1. Deploy `Midnight` and `EcrecoverRatifier` on two local Anvil forks both configured with `chainid = 1` (simulating a chainid collision).
2. On fork A: maker signs an offer tree root; call `EcrecoverRatifier.cancelRoot` is NOT called.
3. Extract `(sig, root, leafIndex, proof)` from fork A.
4. On fork B (same chainid, same contract addresses): call `Midnight.take` with the maker's offer and the extracted `ratifierData`.
5. Assert: `take` succeeds, `consumed[maker][group]` increases on fork B, and a debt position is opened against the maker — without any action by the maker on fork B.
