Audit Report

## Title
Cross-chain signature replay via shared chainId allows unauthorized take on chain B - (File: src/ratifiers/EcrecoverRatifier.sol)

## Summary
`EcrecoverRatifier.isRatified` computes its EIP-712 domain separator solely from `block.chainid` and `address(this)`. When two chains share the same chain ID and the ratifier is deployed at the same address on both (standard with CREATE2), the domain separator is byte-for-byte identical. A taker can replay a maker's chain A offer signature on chain B, executing a take the maker never authorized for that chain.

## Finding Description
**Root cause — `EcrecoverRatifier.sol` line 40:** [1](#0-0) 

The domain separator binds only to `block.chainid` and `address(this)`. The `structHash` is derived from the Merkle root of the offer tree: [2](#0-1) 

The `Offer` struct contains no chain-specific field: [3](#0-2) 

`HashLib.hashOffer` hashes all offer fields, none of which are chain-specific: [4](#0-3) 

Therefore, if `block.chainid` and `address(this)` are identical on two chains, the full digest `keccak256(bytes.concat("\x19\x01", domainSeparator, structHash))` is identical, and a signature valid on chain A is cryptographically valid on chain B.

**Exploit flow (maker is seller, `offer.buy == false`):**
1. Maker uses the protocol on both chain A and chain B, authorizing `EcrecoverRatifier` on both chains via `setIsAuthorized`.
2. Maker signs a sell offer on chain A (intending it only for chain A).
3. Taker observes the offer and signature on chain A.
4. Taker calls `Midnight.take` on chain B with the identical offer struct and signature.
5. `Midnight.take` line 355 checks `isAuthorized[offer.maker][offer.ratifier]` on chain B — passes, because the maker authorized the ratifier on chain B: [5](#0-4) 
6. `EcrecoverRatifier.isRatified` is called on chain B. The domain separator is identical to chain A's. `ecrecover` recovers the maker's address. The signature check passes: [6](#0-5) 
7. The take executes on chain B: debt is created for the maker (seller) on chain B in a market the maker never intended to participate in with that offer.
8. The `SellerIsLiquidatable` check at line 476 passes if the maker has collateral on chain B: [7](#0-6) 

**Why existing checks fail:**
- `isAuthorized[offer.maker][offer.ratifier]`: Not chain-specific; passes if the maker authorized the ratifier on chain B.
- `block.chainid` in the domain separator: Identical on both chains by precondition.
- `address(this)` in the domain separator: Identical on both chains by deterministic deployment.
- `offer.expiry` / `offer.start`: Timestamps, not chain-specific.
- `consumed[offer.maker][offer.group]`: Chain-specific storage, but starts at 0 on chain B — it limits total amount taken but does not reject the replay itself: [8](#0-7) 
- `INITIAL_CHAIN_ID` in `Midnight`: Used only for market IDs to survive hard forks; `EcrecoverRatifier` uses `block.chainid` dynamically, providing no protection when two chains share the same chain ID from genesis: [9](#0-8) 

## Impact Explanation
An unprivileged taker replays a maker's chain A signature on chain B, executing a take the maker never authorized for chain B. The maker (as seller) accrues debt on chain B in a market they did not intend to enter with that offer. If oracle prices on chain B differ from chain A, or the maker's collateral on chain B is insufficient relative to the replayed debt, the position becomes undercollateralized and bad debt is socialized among chain B lenders. This violates the core invariant that offer signatures bind to a specific chain/market/action/amount.

## Likelihood Explanation
**Required preconditions:**
1. Two chains share the same chain ID — a documented real-world occurrence (some L2 testnets have historically shared chain IDs with their mainnet counterparts, or misconfigured chains).
2. `EcrecoverRatifier` deployed at the same address on both chains — near-certain with CREATE2 deterministic deployment, which is standard for protocol deployments.
3. `Midnight` deployed at the same address on both chains — same reasoning.
4. Maker has authorized `EcrecoverRatifier` on chain B's Midnight contract — required; the maker must be an active user on chain B. This is the primary limiting factor.

Conditions 2 and 3 are near-certain for any multi-chain protocol deployment. Condition 1 is the external precondition, but it is a documented real-world occurrence. Condition 4 limits the attack to makers who actively use the protocol on both chains. The replay is repeatable for any offer the maker signs on chain A, up to the `maxUnits`/`maxAssets` cap tracked in chain B's `consumed` mapping.

## Recommendation
Include a chain-specific binding directly in the `Offer` struct (e.g., a `chainId` field hashed into `hashOffer`), so that the offer hash itself is chain-specific regardless of the domain separator. Alternatively, cache the chain ID at construction time (like `INITIAL_CHAIN_ID` in `Midnight`) and use it in the domain separator, and document that the ratifier must not be reused across chains with identical chain IDs. The most robust fix is to add a `chainId` field to the `Offer` struct and validate it in `Midnight.take`, making cross-chain replay structurally impossible even if domain separators collide.

## Proof of Concept
**Fork test plan:**
1. Deploy `Midnight` and `EcrecoverRatifier` at deterministic addresses on two local Anvil forks both configured with `chainId = 1`.
2. On chain A fork: maker calls `setIsAuthorized(ecrecoverRatifier, true)` and signs a sell offer (Merkle tree with one leaf). Record the offer struct, Merkle root, proof, and signature.
3. On chain B fork (same chain ID, same contract addresses): maker calls `setIsAuthorized(ecrecoverRatifier, true)` and deposits collateral.
4. Taker calls `Midnight.take` on chain B fork with the identical offer struct, Merkle proof, and signature from step 2.
5. Assert: the call succeeds, maker's debt on chain B increases, and the maker never signed any offer intended for chain B.
