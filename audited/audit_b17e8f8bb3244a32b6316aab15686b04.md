### Title
Buyer accumulates credit with zero `pendingFee` by taking in small `units` increments due to `mulDivDown` rounding in `take` — (File: src/Midnight.sol)

---

### Summary

In `Midnight.take`, the `buyerPendingFeeIncrease` is computed with `mulDivDown`, rounding the continuous-fee obligation down in favor of the buyer. When `units` is chosen small enough that `buyerCreditIncrease * continuousFee * timeToMaturity < WAD`, the result truncates to zero. Because there is no minimum-`units` guard, a buyer can split any large take into many small calls, each time receiving credit with a `pendingFee` increase of exactly zero, and thereby avoid the continuous fee entirely.

---

### Finding Description

**Root cause — line 385–386 of `src/Midnight.sol`:**

```solidity
uint128 buyerPendingFeeIncrease =
    UtilsLib.toUint128(buyerCreditIncrease.mulDivDown(
        _marketState.continuousFee * timeToMaturity, WAD));
```

`mulDivDown` performs integer division `(x * y) / d`, which truncates toward zero. [1](#0-0) 

`WAD = 1e18`. `MAX_CONTINUOUS_FEE ≈ 317` (i.e., `0.01e18 / 365 days`). [2](#0-1) 

For `buyerPendingFeeIncrease` to truncate to zero the attacker needs:

```
buyerCreditIncrease * continuousFee * timeToMaturity < 1e18
```

At the **maximum** fee (317) and **maximum** TTM (360 days = 31,104,000 s):

```
threshold = 1e18 / (317 * 31,104,000) ≈ 101 units
```

At lower fees or shorter TTM the threshold rises dramatically (e.g., at 1 % of max fee and 1-day TTM the threshold exceeds 3 × 10⁹ units). There is **no minimum-`units` check** in `take`; the only consumed-cap check is an upper bound. [3](#0-2) 

**Exploit flow:**

1. Attacker (as taker/buyer) identifies a market with `continuousFee > 0`.
2. Attacker calls `take` repeatedly with `units ≤ threshold`, each time receiving `buyerCreditIncrease` units of credit with `buyerPendingFeeIncrease = 0`. [4](#0-3) 
3. After N calls the attacker holds `N * units` credit with `pendingFee = 0`.
4. Because `pendingFee = 0`, the `fee` accrued in `updatePositionView` is also zero (`postSlashPendingFee.mulDivDown(...)` = 0), so the attacker's credit is never reduced. [5](#0-4) 
5. At maturity the attacker withdraws the full credit amount, having paid zero continuous fee.

The protocol's own comment acknowledges the direction but treats it as a warning rather than a fix:

> `/// @dev pendingFee updates are rounded in favor of the user. It could lead to fees manipulations too.` [6](#0-5) 

---

### Impact Explanation

The continuous fee is the protocol's primary recurring revenue mechanism; it accrues into `continuousFeeCredit` and is claimable by the `feeClaimer`. [7](#0-6) 

By zeroing out `buyerPendingFeeIncrease` on every small take, the attacker receives credit units at the **face value** of the offer price while paying **none** of the continuous fee that other buyers (who take in normal-sized lots) must pay. The attacker's effective cost of capital is lower than the protocol intends, and the fee claimer receives less revenue than owed. On low-gas chains (L2s) where the cost per `take` call is negligible, the fee evasion can be made arbitrarily complete.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- **No privileged access required** — any taker/buyer can exploit this.
- **No special market conditions required** — any market with `continuousFee > 0` and sufficient TTM is vulnerable.
- **Economically viable on L2s** — on Arbitrum/Base a `take` call costs ~$0.01; avoiding even a few basis points of fee on a large position easily exceeds that cost.
- The attacker only needs a willing maker (or can be the maker themselves via a separate account, since `offer.maker != taker` is the only identity check). [8](#0-7) 

---

### Recommendation

Round `buyerPendingFeeIncrease` **up** instead of down, mirroring the rounding direction already used for `pendingFeeDecrease` in `withdraw` and `sellerPendingFeeDecrease` in `take`:

```solidity
// Before (rounds down — favors buyer):
uint128 buyerPendingFeeIncrease =
    UtilsLib.toUint128(buyerCreditIncrease.mulDivDown(
        _marketState.continuousFee * timeToMaturity, WAD));

// After (rounds up — favors protocol):
uint128 buyerPendingFeeIncrease =
    UtilsLib.toUint128(buyerCreditIncrease.mulDivUp(
        _marketState.continuousFee * timeToMaturity, WAD));
```

This ensures that even a single-unit take incurs at least 1 unit of `pendingFee` when `continuousFee > 0`, eliminating the zero-rounding exploit path. [9](#0-8) 

---

### Proof of Concept

**Setup**: Market with `continuousFee = 317` (max), `timeToMaturity = 31,104,000` (360 days), `buyerPrice = 0.95e18`.

**Threshold**: `1e18 / (317 * 31,104,000) = 101` units.

**Attack**:
```
For i in 1..10_000:
    take(offer, ..., units=100, taker=attacker, ...)
    // buyerCreditIncrease = 100
    // buyerPendingFeeIncrease = (100 * 317 * 31_104_000) / 1e18
    //                         = 985_996_800_000 / 1e18 = 0  ← truncated
```

After 10,000 calls the attacker holds **1,000,000 units** of credit with `pendingFee = 0`.

**Expected `pendingFee`** (if rounded up): `10,000 * 1 = 10,000` units.

**Fee avoided**: At maturity, `updatePositionView` accrues `fee = 0` instead of the correct proportional amount, so the attacker withdraws the full 1,000,000 units. A normal buyer taking 1,000,000 units in one call would have `pendingFee = 985,996` units and their credit would be reduced accordingly before withdrawal. [10](#0-9)
