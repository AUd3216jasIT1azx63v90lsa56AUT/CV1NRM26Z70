### Title
Front-Running of `setConsumed` Forces Execution of Cancelled Offers — (File: src/Midnight.sol)

### Summary
A maker who attempts to cancel an active offer by calling `setConsumed` can be front-run by a taker monitoring the public mempool. The taker executes `take()` against the offer before the cancellation lands, forcing the maker into an unwanted trade. This is the direct analog of the external report's `freezeAccount`/`forceTransfer` front-run pattern: a user-initiated state-change transaction is raced by an adversary to exploit the window between submission and confirmation.

### Finding Description

**Root cause — two independent functions share the same `consumed` mapping with no atomicity guarantee.**

The only on-chain cancellation mechanism for a maker is `setConsumed`: [1](#0-0) 

Setting `amount = type(uint256).max` is the documented way to cancel all offers in a group. However, `take()` reads and increments the same mapping: [2](#0-1) 

There is no lock, no nonce, and no atomic check-and-cancel. The window between the maker's `setConsumed` transaction entering the mempool and it being mined is exploitable.

**Exploit flow:**

1. Maker has a live buy offer (`offer.buy = true`, `maxUnits = N`, `group = G`). The maker has approved `Midnight` to spend their loan token (required for the take to succeed).
2. Market conditions change; maker submits `setConsumed(G, type(uint256).max, maker)` to cancel.
3. Attacker monitors the public mempool, sees the cancellation pending, and immediately submits `take(offer, ratifierData, N, attacker, attacker, address(0), "")` with a higher gas price.
4. Attacker's `take()` is mined first. `consumed[maker][G]` is incremented to `N ≤ maxUnits`, the check passes, and the trade executes.
5. Maker's `setConsumed` mines second — the offer is now cancelled, but the trade already happened.

For a **buy offer** (maker = lender): the maker's loan tokens are pulled via `safeTransferFrom` at line 456, and the maker receives unwanted credit units. [3](#0-2) 

For a **sell offer** (maker = borrower): the maker is forced into debt. The health check at line 476 only reverts if the maker lacks collateral — if the maker already has collateral posted, the debt is silently imposed. [4](#0-3) 

### Impact Explanation

- **Buy offer (maker = lender):** Maker's approved loan tokens are transferred out of their wallet against their will. They receive credit units in a market at a rate they no longer accept. Concrete loss: the difference between the forced lending rate and current market rate, multiplied by the seized assets.
- **Sell offer (maker = borrower):** Maker is forced into a debt position they tried to exit. If collateral is already posted, the debt is created without further consent. The maker must now repay or face liquidation.

Impact is **medium**: direct financial harm to the maker, bounded by the offer's `maxUnits`/`maxAssets`.

### Likelihood Explanation

**Medium.** The attacker requires:
- A mempool monitoring script (widely available, used by MEV bots).
- Enough loan token (or a callback contract) to fund the take — or zero capital if the maker's offer has a maker callback that sources funds.
- A higher gas bid than the maker's cancellation transaction.

All three conditions are realistic for any active MEV participant. The attack is profitable whenever the forced trade has a favorable rate for the attacker (e.g., below-market lending rate).

### Recommendation

1. **Private mempool:** Makers should submit `setConsumed` transactions via a private relay (e.g., Flashbots `eth_sendPrivateTransaction`) so the cancellation is not visible in the public mempool before inclusion.
2. **Short expiry discipline:** Makers should set `offer.expiry` to the shortest acceptable window. An offer with a near-future expiry gives the attacker a smaller race window.
3. **Protocol-level (optional):** Introduce a `cancelOffer` function that atomically sets `consumed` to `type(uint256).max` and emits an event, with documentation explicitly warning that it must be sent via a private relay. This does not eliminate the race but makes the intended usage pattern explicit.

### Proof of Concept

```
Setup:
  - Maker approves Midnight for 1000 USDC.
  - Maker publishes buy offer: maxUnits=1000, group=G, tick=T, expiry=far future.
  - Ratifier is EcrecoverRatifier with maker's valid signature.

Step 1 (maker): broadcast setConsumed(G, type(uint256).max, maker) — gas price 10 gwei.

Step 2 (attacker): on seeing Step 1 in mempool, broadcast
  take(offer, sig, 1000, attacker, attacker, address(0), "") — gas price 15 gwei.

Step 3: attacker's take() mines first.
  consumed[maker][G] += 1000  →  1000 ≤ 1000  ✓
  maker's 1000 USDC pulled via safeTransferFrom → attacker receives credit units.

Step 4: maker's setConsumed mines.
  consumed[maker][G] = type(uint256).max  (too late).

Result: maker lost 1000 USDC into an unwanted lending position.
```
